Key Takeaway : Two key numbers should guide Meta ad decisions: your target Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) and your offer price. First, set a target CAC (e.g., $1,000 for a $2,000/month offer) and define two evaluation points—after spending 2× your target CAC and 1× your offer price. Until those points, ignore early fluctuations like weak leads or no-shows, since they’re often just noise. Most founders fail by reacting too early and killing ads before they have enough data. Only at those two spending thresholds should you decide whether to scale, tweak, or stop a campaign based on real signal, not short-term variance.

Key Takeaway : Meta ad performance can degrade if you train the algorithm on the wrong conversion signals. If you send every lead or booking back to Meta—including low-quality prospects—the system optimizes toward similar low-intent users. This often leads to more volume but worse quality over time. The fix is to improve what you feed into the algorithm: instead of tracking all leads, use tools like the Conversion API to only report qualified, high-intent customers from your CRM. After enough quality conversion signals, the algorithm begins targeting users who resemble your best buyers, improving lead quality significantly. The core idea is that better input data leads to better audience optimization without changing the ad itself.

Key Takeaway : Most B2B SaaS founders overcomplicate ads and waste time on high-production creative, when the simplest format usually performs best: a direct offer ad. This means clearly explaining what the product does, who it’s for, and how it solves a specific pain point—without fancy edits or branding. Instead of polished agency videos, founders often see better results from simple, repeated videos testing different buying angles. The key insight is that clarity converts better than creativity in B2B SaaS. When messaging is straightforward, buyers immediately understand relevance, leading to more qualified calls and lower acquisition costs.
